Single vs Double vs Multiple Entry Schengen Visa — What's the Difference?

📅 Updated: February 2026 ⏱️ 7 min read

When your Schengen visa arrives, it's stamped with one of three entry types: single, double, or multiple. Most applicants don't understand what this means until it's too late — like leaving Schengen for a day trip and discovering their visa is now void. This guide explains exactly what each type means, how to read your visa sticker, and how to improve your chances of getting a multiple entry visa.

How to Read Your Visa Sticker

Your visa sticker has several printed fields. Here are the ones that matter most:

ENTRIES: 01 / 02 / MULT
FROM: [start date of validity]
UNTIL: [end date of validity]
DURATION OF STAY: [max days you can physically be in Schengen]
⚠️ Important distinction: The validity period (FROM–UNTIL) is NOT the same as the duration of stay. A visa valid from 1 March to 30 April with a 30-day duration means you can be inside Schengen for up to 30 days total within that two-month window — not 60 days.

Single Entry Visa — What You Need to Know

A single entry visa is used up the moment you cross into Schengen for the first time. Once you leave — even for a day trip to the UK, Morocco, Turkey, or any non-Schengen country — the visa is void. You cannot re-enter even if your "UNTIL" date hasn't passed and days remain on your duration.

What counts as "leaving Schengen" on a single entry?

  • Flying to the UK, Ireland, or any country outside Schengen
  • A day trip to Morocco from Spain
  • Crossing into Serbia, Albania, or North Macedonia from Greece
  • A cruise that stops in a non-Schengen port

Note: Travelling between Schengen countries (e.g. France → Germany → Spain) does NOT count as an exit. There are no border checks within Schengen for this purpose.

Double Entry Visa — Rarely Issued, Often Misunderstood

A double entry visa (ENTRIES: 02) lets you enter Schengen twice. You could fly in, leave for a week, then come back — and that uses both entries. After your second exit, the visa is void regardless of remaining time.

Double entry visas are not commonly issued. They're occasionally granted when an applicant's itinerary clearly involves entering Schengen, leaving briefly, and returning (for example, a trip that includes a few days in the UK between two Schengen legs).

Multiple Entry Visa — The Flexible Option

A multiple entry visa (ENTRIES: MULT) lets you travel in and out of Schengen freely for the entire validity period. You still cannot exceed 90 days in any 180-day window — but those 90 days can be spread across as many trips as you like.

Example of how MULT works:

  • Validity: 1 January 2026 – 31 December 2026 (1 year)
  • Duration of stay: 90 days
  • You could visit in January (10 days), March (15 days), June (30 days), and September (20 days) = 75 days total with headroom remaining — all perfectly legal

Multiple entry visas are commonly issued with 1-year, 2-year, or 5-year validity. The longer the validity, the stronger your travel and compliance history needs to be.

How to Request a Multiple Entry Visa

You cannot demand a multiple entry visa — it's always at the consulate's discretion. But you can optimise your chances:

  1. State your request explicitly in your cover letter: "I respectfully request a multiple entry visa to facilitate future travel for [reason]."
  2. Show prior Schengen compliance — include copies of previous Schengen visas with entry/exit stamps.
  3. Demonstrate strong ties to your home country — employment, property, family. This shows no overstay risk.
  4. Provide business justification if applicable — regular meetings, conference schedules, client visits.
  5. Apply through a country known to be more generous with multiple entry — Netherlands and Germany have relatively good track records.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Leaving Schengen on a single entry visa expecting to come back — you can't. Once you exit, the visa is void.
  • Confusing validity with duration of stay — a 1-year visa doesn't mean 365 days inside Schengen. Check the "DURATION OF STAY" field.
  • Assuming MULT means unlimited days — it doesn't. You still must respect the 90/180 rule.
  • Not declaring prior Schengen stamps when you apply — VIS records everything, missing or contradicting this raises red flags.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a single entry Schengen visa?

A single entry visa lets you enter Schengen once. As soon as you leave — even briefly — the visa is used up and cannot be re-entered.

What is a multiple entry Schengen visa?

A multiple entry (MULT) visa lets you travel in and out of Schengen freely for the full validity period, subject to the 90/180 day rule on total days inside.

How do I know which type I was issued?

Check the ENTRIES field on your visa sticker: 01 = single, 02 = double, MULT = multiple entry.

Can I ask for a multiple entry visa?

Yes — request it in your cover letter and application form. It's at the consulate's discretion, but prior travel history and strong home ties significantly improve your chances.

Does multiple entry mean unlimited days?

No. You're still limited to 90 days in any 180-day rolling period. Multiple entry just means you can split those days across multiple trips.
